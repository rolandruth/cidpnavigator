## Summary

Plasma exchange (also called plasmapheresis or PLEX) removes harmful antibodies and other inflammatory proteins from the blood by separating and discarding the plasma, then replacing it with albumin or fresh frozen plasma. It is a strongly recommended treatment for CIDP with evidence comparable to IVIG in short-term trials, but its effects are shorter-lived, requiring repeated sessions. It is most useful when IVIG and steroids are contraindicated or ineffective.

---

## Main Content

### How Plasma Exchange Works

Blood is drawn from the patient (through a catheter or large-bore IV), separated in a centrifuge or membrane filter into cellular components (red cells, white cells, platelets) and plasma. The plasma — which contains the pathogenic IgG autoantibodies attacking the myelin — is discarded. The cellular components are returned to the patient along with a replacement fluid (usually 5% human albumin or fresh frozen plasma).

Result: Rapid reduction in circulating IgG and inflammatory mediators, which temporarily suppresses the immune attack on peripheral nerves.

**Key difference from IVIG:** Plasma exchange removes bad antibodies; IVIG suppresses the immune system and speeds clearance of bad antibodies. Both achieve similar short-term results but by different mechanisms.

---

### Indications for Plasma Exchange in CIDP

**Primary indications:**
1. **IVIG contraindicated** — IgA deficiency with anti-IgA antibodies is an absolute contraindication to IVIG; plasma exchange is a major alternative
2. **Steroids contraindicated or failed** — e.g., uncontrolled diabetes, severe osteoporosis, psychiatric history
3. **Both IVIG and steroids failed** — after adequate trials of both, plasma exchange is the next recommended step
4. **Acute severe CIDP** — rapid worsening requiring urgent treatment; plasma exchange can provide quick benefit

---

### Procedure

**Access:**
- **Central venous catheter** (internal jugular, subclavian, or femoral vein): most commonly used; allows high flow rates needed for apheresis
- **Peripheral access**: possible with large-bore IV in both arms; not always achievable; less preferred
- Central lines carry infection and thrombosis risk; proper care is essential

**Acute course (typical):**
- 3–5 exchanges over 1–2 weeks
- Each session: 1–1.5 plasma volumes exchanged (approximately 2.5–4 liters for an average adult)
- Duration per session: 2–4 hours depending on exchange volume and access flow rate
- Setting: hospital apheresis unit or outpatient apheresis clinic

**Maintenance plasma exchange:**
- Some patients require ongoing regular sessions (e.g., every 3–4 weeks) to maintain function
- More logistically demanding than maintenance IVIG or SCIg
- Requires reliable vascular access (often a long-term port or tunneled catheter)

---

### What to Expect During a Session

1. Central line or IV access established (if not already placed)
2. Blood drawn through catheter into apheresis machine
3. Machine separates plasma from blood cells
4. Cells returned with replacement fluid (albumin)
5. Vital signs monitored throughout
6. Process repeated for the full exchange volume
7. Patients often rest during session — bring entertainment
8. May feel cold during procedure (warmed saline/blanket helps)

---

### Side Effects and Risks

**Common (mild):**
- **Tingling around the mouth and fingers** — from citrate anticoagulant binding calcium; treated with IV or oral calcium supplementation
- Lightheadedness or mild hypotension — from volume shifts
- Fatigue after sessions
- Mild nausea

**Moderate:**
- **Hypotension (low blood pressure)** requiring IV fluids
- Catheter-related discomfort or bruising
- Mild allergic reactions to replacement fluids (albumin is well-tolerated; fresh frozen plasma carries higher reaction risk)

**Serious:**
- **Catheter-related infection / sepsis**: Risk from central line; sterile technique essential; monitor for fever, redness, or discharge at catheter site
- **Thrombosis**: Blood clot in catheter or deep vein
- **Bleeding**: Removal of clotting factors during exchange; replaced by albumin, which does not contain clotting factors; risk is usually low but consider fresh frozen plasma if clotting is a concern
- **Hypocalcemia**: Severe muscle cramps, tetany, or cardiac arrhythmia if calcium drops significantly

### Comparison to IVIG

| Feature | Plasma Exchange | IVIG |
|---------|----------------|------|
| Mechanism | Removes pathogenic IgG | Suppresses immune response + speeds IgG clearance |
| Speed of benefit | Similar (days) | Similar (days to 1 week) |
| Duration of effect | Short (weeks) | Weeks (requires maintenance) |
| Setting | Hospital / apheresis clinic | Infusion center or home |
| Side effect burden | Higher (access complications, hypotension, calcium) | Lower (mostly headache, fatigue) |
| IgA deficiency | Safe | Contraindicated |
| Home use | No | Yes (home IVIG) |
| Cost | High | High |
| Evidence | Strong (RCT data) | Strong (multiple RCTs) |

### Maintenance Plasma Exchange

Some patients with severe or refractory CIDP who respond to plasma exchange require ongoing regular sessions to maintain function:

- Typical maintenance interval: every 3–4 weeks (individualized)
- Requires reliable long-term vascular access (tunneled catheter or AV fistula in some cases)
- Quality of life considerations: logistical burden, frequent medical visits, catheter care at home
- Many neurologists will add immunosuppressants to try to extend the maintenance interval or eventually wean plasma exchange

---

## Key Points

- Plasma exchange is strongly recommended for CIDP when IVIG and steroids are contraindicated or fail
- Removes pathogenic IgG antibodies directly from the blood
- Typical acute course: 3–5 sessions over 1–2 weeks; effects last weeks
- Requires central venous access for most patients — carries infection and clot risk
- Citrate-related calcium symptoms (tingling) during sessions are common and easily managed
- IgA deficiency is NOT a contraindication — plasma exchange is the preferred alternative in that scenario
- Not suitable for home use — requires supervised apheresis facility

## Warnings / Contraindications

- Central line infections can be life-threatening — monitor catheter site closely; seek care immediately for fever after plasma exchange
- Avoid fresh frozen plasma as replacement fluid in patients with known allergies to blood products; albumin is preferred
- Hypocalcemia can cause serious cardiac arrhythmias if untreated — calcium supplementation required
- Consult with your neurologist before, during, and after plasma exchange courses
