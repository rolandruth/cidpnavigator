## Summary

Getting IVIG, SCIg, or Vyvgart Hytrulo covered by insurance almost always requires prior authorization (PA). This document explains the prior authorization process, what documentation is needed, how step therapy requirements work, and the difference between Medicare Part B and Part D coverage.

---

## Main Content

### What is Prior Authorization?

Prior authorization (also called "prior auth" or "PA") is a requirement from your insurance company that your doctor get permission before they will cover a specific treatment. For expensive CIDP treatments like IVIG, SCIg, and Vyvgart Hytrulo, PA is almost always required.

Without an approved PA, your insurer will not pay for the treatment. If treatment starts before PA is approved, you may be responsible for the full cost — which can be tens of thousands of dollars.

---

### How the Process Works

1. **Neurologist submits PA request** — includes diagnosis, CIDP documentation, proposed treatment, dosing, and duration
2. **Insurer reviews** — typically 3–14 business days for standard review; 72 hours for urgent
3. **Approved**: treatment can proceed; PA is typically valid for 6–12 months, then must be renewed
4. **Denied**: insurer sends Explanation of Benefits (EOB) with denial reason → appeal process begins

---

### What Documentation Your Neurologist Should Submit

A strong PA request includes:

**Diagnosis documentation:**
- CIDP diagnosis confirmed per 2021 EAN/PNS guidelines
- Nerve conduction study report showing demyelinating features in ≥2 nerves
- Clinical description of symptoms and functional disability (INCAT score, MRC sum score, grip strength)
- Date of symptom onset and duration of disease
- CSF protein level (if available)
- Exclusion of alternative diagnoses

**Treatment justification:**
- Statement that treatment is consistent with EAN/PNS 2021 clinical practice guideline
- FDA-approved indication (for IVIG, SCIg, Vyvgart Hytrulo)
- Prior treatments tried and responses (if any)
- Rationale for the specific treatment selected (e.g., pure motor CIDP — IVIG preferred over steroids)
- Proposed dosing, frequency, and duration

**Supporting references:**
- EAN/PNS 2021 guideline citation (van den Bergh et al., *Eur J Neurol.* 2021;28:3556–3583)
- FDA product label for the specific agent

### Step Therapy ("Fail First")

Many insurers require patients to try less expensive treatments before approving more expensive ones. For CIDP, a common step therapy requirement might be:

*"Patient must fail an adequate trial of corticosteroids before IVIG will be approved."*

**How to document treatment failure:**
- Record dates, doses, and duration of the prior treatment
- Document objective worsening (INCAT score, grip strength, walking ability)
- Note any contraindications that prevented use of the required step (e.g., diabetes → document steroid contraindication)
- Note any adverse effects that required stopping

**Challenging step therapy:**
- Many states have step therapy exception laws — insurer must grant exceptions when:
  - Step therapy drug is contraindicated
  - Patient already tried and failed the step therapy drug
  - Step therapy drug would cause adverse clinical outcome
  - Delay would result in irreversible harm

Your neurologist should document the specific clinical reason the step therapy drug is not appropriate, using language from the EAN/PNS guideline (e.g., "step therapy requires corticosteroids, but patient has pure motor CIDP, for which corticosteroids are contraindicated per EAN/PNS 2021 guidelines as they may cause worsening").

### Medicare Coverage: Part B vs. Part D

| Treatment | Medicare Coverage | Typical Out-of-Pocket |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| IVIG (infusion center or home nurse-administered) | **Part B** (medical benefit) — 80% covered after deductible | 20% coinsurance; Medicare supplement may cover remainder |
| SCIg (Hizentra, Cuvitru) | **Part D** (drug benefit) | Depends on Part D plan formulary and tier |
| Vyvgart Hytrulo | **Part D** or **Part B** (coverage may vary by administration setting) | Depends on plan |

**Key difference:** Part B requires no separate drug plan enrollment and has a standard 80/20 coverage structure. Part D coverage varies significantly by plan — patients should confirm formulary status before starting SCIg.

For home IVIG under Medicare Part B, the drug must be considered "not self-administrable" — IVIG administered by a nurse in the home qualifies.

### Useful Language for Prior Authorization Letters

Your neurologist's PA letter should use precise, clinical language. Phrases that resonate with insurance medical reviewers:

- *"CIDP is a chronic, progressive autoimmune demyelinating polyneuropathy confirmed by nerve conduction studies demonstrating [specific demyelinating features] in [X] nerves."*
- *"Treatment with [IVIG/SCIg/Vyvgart Hytrulo] is medically necessary and consistent with the current international standard of care per the European Academy of Neurology/Peripheral Nerve Society 2021 clinical practice guideline."*
- *"This treatment has an FDA-approved indication for the treatment of adults with CIDP."*
- *"Without this treatment, patient will continue to experience [specific functional losses] with risk of irreversible axonal damage and permanent disability."*
- *"Patient has a documented contraindication to [step therapy drug] due to [specific medical reason]."*
- *"Patient has already failed an adequate trial of [prior treatment] as evidenced by [objective worsening documented on specific dates]."*

## Key Points

- Prior authorization is required for all major CIDP treatments — always submit before starting
- Strong PA requests include: NCS report, symptom documentation with functional scores, guideline citation, and FDA-label reference
- Document step therapy failures objectively with dates, doses, and measurable worsening
- Pure motor CIDP patients should document corticosteroid contraindication explicitly
- IVIG = Medicare Part B; SCIg = Medicare Part D — different cost-sharing structures
- Manufacturer patient support programs can help bridge cost gaps during approval delays

## Warnings / Contraindications

- Do not start expensive treatments before PA is approved unless there is a medical emergency — unauthorized treatment may not be covered retroactively
- Prior authorization approval does not guarantee payment — verify dosing approved matches what will be administered
