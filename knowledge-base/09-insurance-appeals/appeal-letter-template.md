## Summary

When insurance denies coverage for IVIG, SCIg, or Vyvgart Hytrulo, patients have the right to appeal. This document provides appeal letter templates and the key medical necessity arguments your neurologist should use. Strong appeals are concise, evidence-based, and cite the EAN/PNS guideline and FDA approval.

*This template is for the treating neurologist to submit on the patient's behalf. Fill in [BRACKETS].*

Re: **Appeal of Denial of Prior Authorization for IVIG**
Member Name: [PATIENT NAME]

Claim/Reference Number: [NUMBER]
Treating Physician: [NEUROLOGIST NAME, MD]

Dear Medical Director,

I am writing to appeal the denial of prior authorization for intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) for my patient, [PATIENT NAME], who carries a confirmed diagnosis of **Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy (CIDP)** (ICD-10: G61.81).

**Diagnosis and clinical basis:**

[PATIENT NAME] has been diagnosed with CIDP confirmed by:
- Nerve conduction studies performed on [DATE] demonstrating demyelinating features in [X] nerves, including [list nerves], consistent with the 2021 EAN/PNS diagnostic criteria
- Clinical presentation of [describe: symmetric proximal and distal weakness, sensory loss, areflexia] with onset [DATE] and progression over [X] weeks/months
- [Add: CSF protein [X] mg/dL; MRI findings; INCAT score X/10; grip strength X kg bilateral]
- Exclusion of CIDP mimics including POEMS syndrome, anti-MAG neuropathy, hereditary neuropathy, and other causes

**Treatment necessity:**

IVIG is the medically necessary, FDA-approved, first-line treatment for this patient's CIDP. Treatment is consistent with the current international standard of care as established by the **European Academy of Neurology/Peripheral Nerve Society (EAN/PNS) 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline**, which gives a strong (Level A) recommendation for IVIG as initial treatment for typical CIDP (van den Bergh et al., *Eur J Neurol.* 2021;28(11):3556–3583).

IVIG holds FDA approval for the treatment of CIDP. The FDA-approved indication specifically names CIDP as an indication for [PRODUCT NAME, e.g., Gamunex-C, Privigen].

**[IF STEP THERAPY DENIAL — add this paragraph:]**
*The denial cites a requirement to trial corticosteroids prior to IVIG approval. This step therapy requirement is clinically inappropriate for this patient for the following reasons: [choose applicable]*
- *Patient has pure motor CIDP, for which corticosteroids are contraindicated per EAN/PNS 2021 guideline, as they may worsen the condition*
- *Patient has [diabetes / severe osteoporosis / psychiatric history / uncontrolled hypertension] which represents a contraindication to corticosteroid use*
- *Patient has already failed an adequate trial of oral prednisone [X mg/day for X weeks], with objective worsening documented by [INCAT/MRC/grip strength] on [DATE]*

**Consequences of denial:**

CIDP is a progressive autoimmune neuropathy. Untreated or undertreated CIDP leads to permanent axonal damage, irreversible weakness, and disability. Delay in appropriate treatment risks permanent neurological harm that cannot be reversed with later treatment. This patient's current functional status [describe: difficulty walking, inability to perform X, fall risk] demonstrates the urgent need for treatment.

**Request:**

I request immediate approval of IVIG at [X g/kg loading dose over X days] followed by maintenance [X g/kg every X weeks] for [X months/duration]. I am available for a peer-to-peer review call to discuss this case directly.

Respectfully,

[NEUROLOGIST NAME, MD]
[Practice Name]

*Use when SCIg (Hizentra or Cuvitru) is denied as maintenance therapy.*

---

**[DATE]**

Re: **Appeal of Denial for Subcutaneous Immunoglobulin (SCIg) — [HIZENTRA / CUVITRU]**

Dear Medical Director,

I am writing to appeal the denial of [Hizentra / Cuvitru] for my patient [PATIENT NAME] with confirmed CIDP (ICD-10: G61.81).

[Hizentra / Cuvitru] holds **FDA approval** for the maintenance treatment of adults with CIDP. This patient has been stable on IV IVIG maintenance at [X g/kg every X weeks] for [X months/years]. Transition to SCIg is medically appropriate because:

1. Patient has demonstrated clinical stability on immunoglobulin maintenance therapy
2. SCIg provides equivalent therapeutic benefit with improved tolerability (fewer systemic reactions) and greater patient independence
3. The 2021 EAN/PNS guideline recommends subcutaneous immunoglobulin as equivalent to IV IVIG for CIDP maintenance
4. Home SCIg reduces infusion-related adverse events and avoids the peak-and-trough effect that this patient experiences with IV dosing (specifically: [describe wearing off symptoms])

The proposed SCIg dose has been calculated from the patient's current IVIG maintenance dose using the standard conversion factor.

I request approval for [Hizentra / Cuvitru] at [X g/week] subcutaneously.

[NEUROLOGIST signature block]

### Key Medical Necessity Arguments (Reference List)

Use these arguments in any CIDP insurance appeal:

**Guideline support:**
> "The EAN/PNS 2021 guideline gives a strong (Level A) recommendation for IVIG as initial treatment for CIDP. This is the current international standard of care." — cite: van den Bergh et al., *Eur J Neurol.* 2021;28(11):3556–3583

**FDA approval:**
> "[Product name] is FDA-approved for the treatment of adults with CIDP. This is not an off-label use."

**Irreversibility argument:**
> "CIDP causes axonal degeneration with delayed or inadequate treatment. Axonal loss is not reversible. Early adequate treatment prevents permanent disability."

**Functional impact:**
> "Patient's current INCAT score of [X] indicates [describe functional level]. Without treatment, further deterioration will lead to [specific functional losses]."

**Contraindication to step therapy:**
> "Step therapy requiring corticosteroids is contraindicated for this patient due to [pure motor CIDP / medical comorbidity]. The EAN/PNS guideline specifically warns against corticosteroids in pure motor CIDP."

### Peer-to-Peer Review

When a written appeal is denied, request a **peer-to-peer (P2P) review** — a phone call between your neurologist and the insurance company's medical director.

**How to request:**
- Call the insurance company's provider line (number on denial letter)
- Ask specifically for "peer-to-peer review" or "physician-to-physician review"
- Schedule within the timeframe specified in the denial letter (usually 14–30 days)

**What the neurologist should cover:**
- Specific NCS findings and why they meet EAN/PNS demyelination criteria
- Why the requested treatment is appropriate for this specific patient
- Why step therapy alternatives are contraindicated or have already failed
- The functional consequences of delayed treatment

P2P reviews are often more successful than written appeals for complex cases — the insurance medical director hears the clinical nuance directly.

---

### External/Independent Review

If all internal appeals are exhausted, patients have the right to an **External (Independent) Review** under the Affordable Care Act.

- Request through your state insurance commissioner's office
- An independent organization reviews the denial — the insurer must abide by the decision
- This is a patient right and does not require an attorney
- Find your state commissioner at: naic.org/consumer_state_insurance_departments.htm

## Key Points

- Always get the denial reason in writing before appealing
- Appeal within the timeframe on the denial letter (typically 30–180 days)
- Include: NCS report, clinical notes, guideline citation, FDA approval, functional impact statement
- Step therapy denials can be overcome with: contraindication documentation, failure documentation, or state step therapy exception law
- Peer-to-peer review is often more effective than written appeals for complex neurology cases
- External independent review is available when internal appeals fail

## Warnings / Contraindications

- Appeals have strict deadlines — do not wait; file within the denial letter timeframe
- If treatment is medically urgent, ask specifically for an **expedited appeal** (decision within 72 hours)
