## Summary

SCIg (subcutaneous immunoglobulin) delivers the same IgG antibodies as IVIG but through small injections under the skin rather than into a vein. FDA-approved brands Hizentra (20%) and Cuvitru (20%) are indicated for CIDP maintenance. SCIg offers patients independence, steady blood levels without peaks and troughs, and fewer systemic side effects than IVIG — making it an increasingly preferred maintenance option for patients who are stable on IVIG.

Both are equivalent in efficacy. Choice is usually driven by insurance coverage, individual tolerability, or patient preference.

### How SCIg Differs from IVIG

SCIg delivers the same immunoglobulin into subcutaneous tissue (the fat layer under the skin). From there, IgG is slowly absorbed into the bloodstream over 24–72 hours, producing a steady-state serum IgG level without the large peak-and-trough pattern of IV infusion.

**Clinical implication:** Patients on SCIg typically do NOT experience the "wearing off" phenomenon common with IVIG maintenance.

---

### Dosing

**Conversion from IVIG to SCIg:**
- The total monthly dose of IgG is maintained, divided into more frequent smaller doses
- Dose conversion factor: SCIg dose is typically 1.37× the equivalent IVIG dose (to account for lower bioavailability of subcutaneous route)
- Your pharmacist or neurologist will calculate your individual SCIg dose

**Typical schedule:**
- Weekly SCIg: small volume injected over 1–2 hours each week
- Biweekly: slightly larger volume every 2 weeks
- Volume per site: typically 20–40 mL per injection site; multiple sites used simultaneously

**Example:**
- Patient on IVIG 1 g/kg every 3 weeks (total ~70 g/month for a 70 kg patient)
- Equivalent SCIg: ~95 g/month divided into weekly injections of ~24 g each

### How to Self-Administer SCIg

**Equipment:**
- Infusion pump (provided by specialty pharmacy) OR manual push ("subcutaneous push" method)
- Butterfly needles or specialized SCIg needle sets
- Alcohol swabs, gauze, sterile dressing
- Drug, tubing, pump supplies

**Injection sites (rotate each infusion):**
- Abdomen (most common — largest surface area)
- Upper thighs
- Upper outer arms
- Lower back / flanks
- Rotate sites to prevent lipohypertrophy (fatty lumps from repeated injections)

**Procedure (simplified):**
1. Wash hands thoroughly
2. Allow drug to reach room temperature (30 min out of refrigerator)
3. Clean injection sites with alcohol swab, allow to dry
4. Insert needles at ~45° angle into subcutaneous tissue
5. Connect tubing to pump; start infusion at low rate
6. Monitor for local reactions; increase rate if tolerated
7. After infusion, remove needles, apply gentle pressure

**Training:**
- First administration always done under nurse supervision
- Specialty pharmacy or home health nurse provides full training
- Practice sessions scheduled before going fully independent

### Advantages Over IVIG

| Advantage | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Home self-administration | No clinic visits for routine maintenance |
| Steady IgG levels | No "wearing off" between doses |
| Fewer systemic reactions | Headache, chills, nausea far less common |
| Smaller volumes per session | Less cardiovascular burden |
| Schedule flexibility | Weekly timing can be adjusted (not locked to clinic schedule) |
| Patient independence | Travel, work, lifestyle easier to manage |
| Fewer venipunctures | No IV line — important for patients with poor venous access |

---

### Side Effects

**Local (most common and generally mild):**
- Redness, swelling, warmth, itching, and firmness at injection sites
- Usually resolves within 24 hours
- Improves with regular site rotation and slower infusion rates
- Rarely require stopping treatment

**Systemic (much less common than with IVIG):**
- Headache (less frequent and less severe than IVIG)
- Fatigue
- Mild nausea
- Allergic reactions are rare

---

### Who Is a Good Candidate for SCIg?

**Best candidates:**
- Patients who are stable on IVIG and want more independence
- Patients with poor venous access (SCIg avoids IV lines)
- Patients who experience significant "wearing off" at end of IVIG cycle
- Patients who prefer home-based treatment
- Patients with difficulty scheduling regular clinic infusion appointments
- Patients who travel frequently

**May not be ideal if:**
- Patient is not yet stable (SCIg not typically used for induction)
- Patient has significant fear of needles or dexterity limitations
- Caregiver/patient not able to safely administer at home
- Significant skin conditions at injection sites

---

### Transition from IVIG to SCIg

1. Neurologist determines patient is stable on IVIG and approves transition
2. Dose calculation performed (IVIG monthly dose × 1.37 conversion factor)
3. Prescriptionwritten and sent to specialty pharmacy
4. Specialty pharmacy delivers drug, pump, and supplies to patient's home
5. Home nurse or pharmacy nurse provides training session
6. First home infusion done with nurse present
7. Patient self-administers thereafter; check-in with neurologist at 4–8 weeks

### Insurance Coverage Differences

This is an important practical difference between IVIG and SCIg:

| | IVIG | SCIg |
|--|------|------|
| Medicare coverage | **Part B** (medical benefit, 80% after deductible) | **Part D** (pharmacy benefit — different formulary, co-pays) |
| Private insurance | Medical benefit, prior auth required | Pharmacy benefit, prior auth required |
| Out-of-pocket cost | Co-pay under Part B | May require Part D supplemental coverage |
| Cost-sharing | Lower with good Medicare supplement | Varies widely |

**Key point:** Some patients find SCIg more or less expensive than IVIG depending on their specific insurance plan. Discuss both options with your neurologist and insurance coordinator.

Patient assistance programs:
- CSL Behring (Hizentra): COMPASS support program
- Takeda (Cuvitru): patient support programs available

## Key Points

- SCIg delivers the same IgG as IVIG but subcutaneously (under skin), at home, by the patient
- FDA approved for CIDP maintenance: Hizentra and Cuvitru (both 20%, approved 2018)
- Weekly small injections replace monthly large IV infusions
- Steady IgG levels eliminate "wearing off" that many IVIG patients experience
- Local injection site reactions (redness, swelling) are common but mild; systemic reactions are rare
- Covered under Medicare Part D (pharmacy benefit) — different from IVIG's Part B coverage
- Transition from IVIG to SCIg requires dose recalculation and training

## Warnings / Contraindications

- Same contraindications as IVIG — screen for IgA deficiency before first use
- Do not use SCIg for CIDP induction (insufficient evidence) — IVIG IV is used for loading doses
- Proper training is essential — incorrect technique risks infection, inadequate absorption, or injury
- Consult your neurologist before transitioning from IVIG to SCIg
