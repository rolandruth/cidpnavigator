## Summary

IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin) is the most commonly used first-line treatment for CIDP and the only treatment with multiple regulatory approvals worldwide for this indication. It is pooled human immunoglobulin (IgG antibodies) derived from thousands of plasma donors, given by IV infusion. It does not cure CIDP but suppresses the immune attack on myelin and typically needs to be repeated on a schedule to maintain benefit.

---

## Main Content

### How IVIG Works

IVIG modulates the immune system through multiple mechanisms:
- Saturates the FcRn receptor (the neonatal Fc receptor that recycles IgG), accelerating clearance of the pathogenic autoantibodies attacking myelin
- Provides anti-idiotype antibodies that neutralize disease-causing antibodies
- Modulates complement activation and cytokine production
- Reduces inflammatory T and B cell activity

The result: temporary suppression of the immune attack on myelin, allowing nerve function to stabilize or improve.

**Important:** IVIG does not repair already-damaged myelin. Recovery of function depends on the nerve's own remyelination capacity over weeks to months after treatment.

### Standard Dosing Protocol

**Induction (loading) dose:**
- 2 g/kg total body weight over 2–5 consecutive days
- Example: 70 kg patient → 140 g total IVIG
- Divided: 28 g/day × 5 days OR 35 g/day × 4 days OR 70 g/day × 2 days
- Typical infusion time: 4–8 hours per day depending on rate tolerance

**When to expect benefit:**
- Many patients notice improvement within 3–7 days of completing induction
- Full response assessed at 4–6 weeks
- If no response after two full induction courses → reconsider diagnosis or consider alternative treatment

**Maintenance dose:**
- 1 g/kg every 3 weeks, OR
- 2 g/kg every 6 weeks
- Titrated to the **lowest dose and longest interval** that maintains function
- Some patients require higher frequency (every 2 weeks) if they "wear off" early
- Some stable patients can be stretched to every 8–12 weeks

### What to Expect During Infusion

**Before infusion:**
- Pre-medications often given: acetaminophen (Tylenol), diphenhydramine (Benadryl), and/or hydration with saline
- Rate starts slow (usually 0.5 mg/kg/min), increased gradually over 30–60 minutes
- Full infusion typically takes 4–8 hours depending on dose and rate tolerance

**During infusion:**
- Sit or recline comfortably — bring entertainment, water, snacks
- Nurse monitors vital signs (BP, heart rate, temperature) periodically
- Alert staff immediately for: headache, chills, flushing, chest tightness, dizziness

**After infusion:**
- Drink plenty of water for 24–48 hours (prevents thrombotic and renal complications)
- Headache in the 24–48 hours after is common
- Fatigue is normal for 1–3 days post-infusion
- Many patients feel improvement in strength and sensation within days to a week

### Side Effects

**Common (mild):**
- Headache (most common; often treated with premedication)
- Fatigue and flu-like symptoms for 1–3 days after infusion
- Chills, fever, flushing during infusion
- Nausea
- Back or joint pain
- Local vein irritation

**Less common (moderate):**
- **Aseptic meningitis**: Severe headache, stiff neck, photosensitivity within 24 hours — usually resolves in days; treat with steroids if severe
- Hemolysis (breakdown of red blood cells) — more common with large doses, blood type A or B
- Skin reactions (rash, hives)

**Rare but serious:**
- **Thrombosis** (blood clots): DVT, stroke, MI, pulmonary embolism — risk is higher in patients with cardiovascular risk factors, high IgG levels, or rapid infusion; stay hydrated, infuse slowly
- **Renal failure**: Especially with sucrose-containing formulations (older products); current 10% formulations are safer; ensure adequate hydration
- **Severe allergic reaction / anaphylaxis**: Rare; risk is highest in patients with **IgA deficiency** — must screen before first infusion

---

### Contraindications

- **IgA deficiency with anti-IgA antibodies**: Absolute contraindication — risk of severe anaphylaxis. Screen all patients with serum IgA before first IVIG infusion. If IgA deficient, use plasma exchange or corticosteroids instead.
- **Severe renal impairment**: Use with caution; ensure adequate hydration; avoid sucrose-containing products
- **Hypercoagulable states / recent thrombosis**: Use with caution; consider prophylactic anticoagulation

---

### Home Infusion vs. Infusion Center

**Infusion center:**
- Required for first few infusions until tolerability established
- Better monitoring during infusion
- Staffed nursing care available for reactions

**Home IVIG:**
- Available after tolerability confirmed
- Nurse comes to patient's home
- More convenient; reduces travel burden
- May require additional insurance approval
- Not all patients are candidates (must have reliable venous access, stable clinical status)

---

### Cost and Access

- IVIG is extremely expensive: typically **$10,000–$20,000 or more per infusion** in the United States
- Covered under **Medicare Part B** (medical benefit) when medically necessary for CIDP
- Private insurance: requires prior authorization and often step therapy
- Home IVIG vs. infusion center: coverage varies by payer; some require facility infusion
- Patient assistance programs: manufacturer programs, GBS/CIDP Foundation financial assistance
- If cost is a barrier: discuss SCIg with your neurologist (may be covered under Part D / pharmacy benefit differently)

---

### IVIG vs. SCIg: Key Differences

| Feature | IVIG | SCIg |
|---------|------|------|
| Route | IV (into vein) | SC (under skin) |
| Setting | Infusion center or home nurse | Self-administered at home |
| Frequency | Every 3–6 weeks | Weekly or every 2 weeks |
| Volume per session | Large (several hundred mL over hours) | Small (typically <200 mL/session) |
| Serum IgG levels | Peaks and troughs | Steady state |
| "Wearing off" | Common (trough effect) | Rare |
| Systemic reactions | More common | Much less common |
| Insurance | Part B (medical) | Part D (pharmacy) |

SCIg is an excellent maintenance option for patients who are stable on IVIG and want more independence. The transition is typically done under neurologist supervision.

## Key Points

- IVIG is FDA-approved for CIDP; loading dose is 2 g/kg, maintenance is 1 g/kg every 3 weeks
- Works by temporarily suppressing the immune attack on myelin — must be repeated
- Benefit typically felt within days to 1 week after infusion
- Pre-screen all patients for IgA deficiency before first infusion
- Most common side effects: headache, fatigue, flu-like symptoms — usually mild and temporary
- Stay hydrated before and after infusion to reduce clot and kidney risk
- "Wearing off" near the end of the dosing cycle suggests dose or interval adjustment needed
- SCIg is a home-based alternative that avoids peaks and troughs

## Warnings / Contraindications

- **IgA deficiency with anti-IgA antibodies**: do not give IVIG — risk of fatal anaphylaxis
- **Thrombosis risk**: slow infusion, maintain hydration, caution in cardiovascular risk patients
- **Aseptic meningitis**: severe post-infusion headache should be evaluated; usually benign but watch for worsening
- This information is educational only — dosing decisions must be made by your neurologist
