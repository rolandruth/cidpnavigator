## Summary

The GBS|CIDP Foundation International is the leading patient advocacy organization for CIDP. This overview explains what CIDP is in plain language, its causes, how it differs from GBS, and the available treatment options. This is a strong foundational document for patients newly diagnosed or researching CIDP.

## Main Content

### What is CIDP?

CIDP (Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy) is a rare disorder of the peripheral nerves characterized by gradually increasing sensory loss and weakness associated with loss of reflexes.

- Affects approximately **1–2 new cases per 100,000 people per year**
- Prevalence may reach **9 per 100,000** in some regions
- Can develop at **any age**
- More frequent in **males than females**
- CIDP is **chronic** — unlike GBS (Guillain-Barré syndrome), which is acute and typically resolves; CIDP either continues to progress or relapses and remits over time

### What Causes CIDP?

CIDP results from immune system dysfunction. The immune system incorrectly identifies myelin (the protective sheath around nerve fibers) as foreign and attacks it.

- Myelin normally acts as electrical insulation around nerve axons, enabling efficient signal transmission from the brain to the body and back
- When myelin is damaged, electrical impulses slow or fail — causing weakness and sensory loss
- The exact trigger that causes the immune system to attack myelin in CIDP is not fully understood
- CIDP is **not contagious, not hereditary** (though rare genetic factors may predispose some individuals), and not caused by lifestyle factors

### How is CIDP Different from GBS?

Both CIDP and GBS are demyelinating conditions caused by immune attack on peripheral nerves, but they differ in important ways:

| Feature | CIDP | GBS |
|---------|------|-----|
| Onset | Gradual (over 8+ weeks) | Rapid (peaks within 4 weeks) |
| Course | Chronic — progressive or relapsing | Usually monophasic (one episode) |
| Recovery | Variable; often requires ongoing treatment | Most patients recover with supportive care |
| Treatment | Long-term maintenance therapy | Short-term treatment; mainly supportive |

### Diagnosis

CIDP diagnosis is based on:
1. **Symptoms**: Progressive weakness and sensory loss in both arms and legs over 8+ weeks
2. **Nerve conduction studies and EMG**: Tests showing demyelination in peripheral nerves
3. **Spinal fluid analysis**: Usually shows elevated protein with normal cell count
4. **Blood and urine tests**: To rule out other conditions

### Treatment Options

The Foundation describes three established first-line treatments:

**1. Corticosteroids (Prednisone, Prednisolone)**
- Oral anti-inflammatory medications that mimic naturally occurring hormones
- Can improve strength and reduce inflammation
- Side effects may be limiting with long-term use (weight gain, bone loss, elevated glucose)

**2. High-dose IVIG (Intravenous Immune Globulins)**
- The only treatment with FDA, Canadian, and European approval for CIDP
- Provides healthy antibodies that modify the immune response
- Given by IV infusion; requires regular maintenance infusions

**3. Plasma Exchange (PE/PLEX)**
- Removes blood plasma (which contains harmful antibodies) and replaces it
- Can provide rapid benefit
- Effects are short-lived; requires repeated sessions

Second and third-line medications are used when first-line options don't work well enough or cause intolerable side effects.

### CIDP Variants

The Foundation recognizes several CIDP phenotypes:
- **Multifocal Motor Neuropathy (MMN)**: Asymmetric pure motor neuropathy (note: distinct from CIDP)
- **Lewis-Sumner syndrome (MADSAM)**: Sensory-motor, asymmetric, multifocal
- **Pure sensory CIDP**: Sensory loss without weakness
- **Pure motor CIDP**: Weakness without sensory loss (important: steroid caution)

## Key Points

- CIDP is a chronic, immune-mediated peripheral nerve disorder causing progressive weakness and sensory loss
- It is not contagious, not hereditary in most cases, and not caused by lifestyle choices
- Prevalence: approximately 1–9 per 100,000 people
- Diagnosis requires nerve conduction testing, typically also CSF protein and labs to exclude mimics
- Three established first-line treatments: IVIG, corticosteroids, plasma exchange
- GBS/CIDP Foundation is the leading patient support organization — free resources and Health Navigator consultations available
