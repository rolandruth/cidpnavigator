## Summary

The 2021 EAN/PNS guideline (second revision) provides the current international standard for diagnosing CIDP. It simplifies certainty levels from three (definite/probable/possible) to two (CIDP and possible CIDP), replaces "atypical CIDP" with named "CIDP variants," and adds treatment response as a supportive diagnostic criterion.

Published in: *European Journal of Neurology* 2021;28(11):3556–3583 and *Journal of the Peripheral Nervous System* 2021;26(3):242–268.

---

## Main Content

### Diagnostic Framework

CIDP diagnosis requires all three of:
1. **Clinical presentation** consistent with typical CIDP or a recognized CIDP variant
2. **Electrodiagnostic evidence** of peripheral nerve demyelination in ≥2 nerves
3. **Exclusion** of other causes of demyelinating neuropathy

Certainty levels:
- **CIDP**: Clinical + electrodiagnostic criteria met
- **Possible CIDP**: Clinical criteria met but electrodiagnostic criteria only partially met; diagnosis supported by ≥1 supportive criterion

---

### Clinical Criteria — Typical CIDP

All of the following:
- Chronic progressive, stepwise, or recurrent symmetric proximal **and** distal limb weakness **and** sensory dysfunction of more than one limb
- Reduced or absent deep tendon reflexes in the affected limbs
- Progression over **at least 8 weeks**
- Upper limb involvement (distinguishes from MMN and length-dependent neuropathies)

---

### CIDP Variants

These are distinct phenotypes, not "atypical CIDP." Each variant has its own clinical criteria.

**1. Distal CIDP (DADS — distal acquired demyelinating symmetric)**
- Predominantly distal weakness and sensory loss
- Absent or reduced reflexes distally
- No or minimal proximal involvement
- Note: DADS with anti-MAG antibodies is a separate entity, not CIDP

**2. Multifocal CIDP (Lewis-Sumner Syndrome / MADSAM)**
- Asymmetric sensorimotor involvement in distribution of individual nerves
- ≥2 limbs affected
- Electrodiagnostic evidence of multifocal demyelination
- Note: must distinguish from MMN (multifocal motor neuropathy)

**3. Focal CIDP**
- Involvement of only one limb, or only one plexus (brachial or lumbosacral), or only one section of the peripheral nervous system
- Electrodiagnostic evidence of demyelination restricted to that area

**4. Pure Motor CIDP**
- Only motor involvement — no sensory symptoms or signs
- **Important**: corticosteroids may worsen pure motor CIDP — use IVIG preferentially

**5. Pure Sensory CIDP**
- Only sensory involvement — no objective weakness
- Reflexes reduced or absent
- Electrodiagnostic criteria may be difficult to meet in pure sensory

### Electrodiagnostic Criteria (Nerve Conduction Studies)

Demyelinating features must be present in ≥2 motor nerves. For each feature, values must exceed the defined threshold (upper limit of normal × defined factor) in the relevant nerve.

**Demyelinating features (any one of the following per nerve):**

| Feature | Threshold (approximate) |
|---------|------------------------|
| Prolonged distal motor latency (DML) | > 1.5× upper limit of normal (ULN) |
| Reduced motor nerve conduction velocity (MNCV) | < 0.7× lower limit of normal (LLN) |
| Prolonged or absent F-wave latency | > 1.3× ULN (or absent) |
| Partial motor conduction block | ≥30% reduction in compound muscle action potential (CMAP) area proximal vs distal |
| Abnormal temporal dispersion | ≥30% increase in CMAP duration proximal vs distal |

Median, ulnar, peroneal, and tibial nerves are the standard nerves tested.

**Note:** Sural nerve sparing with abnormal upper limb nerves ("sural sparing pattern") supports CIDP over axonal or length-dependent neuropathy.

---

### Supportive Criteria

When electrodiagnostic criteria are not fully met, ≥1 of the following supports a diagnosis of **Possible CIDP**:

1. **CSF protein elevation** > 0.45 g/L (45 mg/dL) with normal cell count (< 10 cells/μL)
2. **MRI findings**: Gadolinium enhancement or hypertrophy of nerve roots, cauda equina, lumbosacral or cervical plexus
3. **Nerve ultrasound**: Cross-sectional area enlargement of peripheral nerves at non-entrapment sites
4. **Treatment response**: Objectively documented improvement with IVIG, plasma exchange, or corticosteroids (≥1 point on INCAT disability scale, or documented improvement on grip strength, MRC sum score, or timed up-and-go)
5. **Nerve biopsy**: Onion bulb formation, demyelination/remyelination on teased fiber analysis, or inflammatory infiltrates in endoneurium

---

### Exclusion Criteria

CIDP diagnosis should **not** be made if there is evidence of:

- Borrelia burgdorferi infection (Lyme neuroborreliosis)
- Diphtheria
- Drug toxicity with demyelinating potential (e.g., amiodarone, chloroquine)
- Hereditary demyelinating neuropathy (CMT1, CMT4, HNPP) — genetic testing may be needed
- POEMS syndrome (with M-protein, organomegaly, endocrinopathy, skin changes)
- Osteosclerotic myeloma
- Lymphoma or leukemia with direct nerve infiltration
- Anti-MAG neuropathy (predominantly distal sensory variant — treat differently from CIDP)
- Diabetic neuropathy (unless concurrent CIDP is strongly suspected and well-supported)
- Vasculitic neuropathy
- Sarcoidosis
- Amyloidosis (e.g., TTR amyloidosis — important to exclude in late-onset neuropathy)

### Diagnostic Workup Checklist

**Baseline blood tests:**
- CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, HbA1c
- Serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP) + immunofixation (to detect M-protein)
- Anti-MAG antibodies (if predominantly distal/sensory)
- VEGF (if POEMS suspected)
- Anti-CNTN1, anti-CASPR1, anti-NF155 antibodies (paranodopathy panel — treatment implications)
- Vitamin B12, folate
- TSH
- HIV, hepatitis B/C (if clinically indicated)
- ANA, ANCA (if vasculitis suspected)
- Lyme serology (if exposure risk)

**Nerve conduction studies + EMG** (minimum: bilateral median, ulnar, peroneal, tibial, sural)

**Lumbar puncture** (CSF protein + cell count): recommended if diagnosis uncertain

**MRI** (lumbosacral spine + plexus, or cervical, with gadolinium): if diagnosis uncertain or focal symptoms

**Nerve biopsy**: rarely needed; consider for atypical cases or if paranodopathy panel positive

---

### Autoantibodies and Paranodopathies

A subset of patients previously diagnosed as CIDP have autoantibodies against nodal/paranodal proteins. These are now considered a distinct group ("paranodopathies") with different treatment responses:

| Antibody | Association | Treatment note |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Anti-NF155 (neurofascin-155) | Young onset, ataxia, tremor, distal predominance, poor IVIG response | Rituximab may be effective |
| Anti-CNTN1 (contactin-1) | Rapid onset, severe, older patients, nephrotic syndrome possible | Poor IVIG response; rituximab or steroids |
| Anti-CASPR1 | Severe, painful, neuropathic pain | Rituximab may be effective |
| Anti-NF140/186 | Variable | Variable |

**Clinical implication:** IVIG often fails in paranodopathies. Testing for these antibodies is recommended for atypical presentations or IVIG non-responders.

## Key Points

- Diagnosis requires clinical features + electrophysiology in ≥2 nerves + exclusion of mimics
- Two certainty levels: CIDP (definite) and Possible CIDP (supportive criteria only)
- Five named variants replace "atypical CIDP": distal, multifocal, focal, pure motor, pure sensory
- Pure motor CIDP may worsen with corticosteroids — flag for treating neurologist
- Anti-MAG neuropathy is NOT CIDP — do not treat as CIDP
- Paranodopathy antibodies (anti-NF155, anti-CNTN1, anti-CASPR1) predict IVIG resistance
- Average time from symptom onset to CIDP diagnosis is 3–5 years; underdiagnosis is common
- Treatment response can itself serve as a supportive diagnostic criterion

## Warnings / Contraindications

- Do not diagnose CIDP without electrodiagnostic evidence of demyelination (or at minimum one supportive criterion for Possible CIDP)
- Do not start corticosteroids in suspected pure motor CIDP without neurologist guidance — risk of worsening
- Anti-MAG-associated neuropathy does not respond to standard CIDP treatment; treating as CIDP may delay appropriate management
