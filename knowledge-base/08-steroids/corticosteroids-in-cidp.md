## Summary

Corticosteroids (prednisone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone) are first-line treatment for CIDP and have been used for decades. They broadly suppress the immune system, reducing the inflammation that damages myelin. They are inexpensive and effective (~60–70% response rate), but long-term use carries significant side effects that require active monitoring and management. Corticosteroids should NOT be used as first-line treatment for pure motor CIDP, where they may worsen the condition.

---

## Main Content

### How Corticosteroids Work in CIDP

Corticosteroids suppress the immune system broadly:
- Reduce production of pro-inflammatory cytokines
- Decrease lymphocyte (T and B cell) activity
- Reduce macrophage-mediated myelin damage
- Decrease antibody production over time

Unlike IVIG (which works within days), steroids often take **weeks to months** for full benefit — but their effects can last longer once established.

---

### Corticosteroid Protocols Used in CIDP

**1. Daily oral prednisone (most common first-line approach):**
- Starting dose: 1 mg/kg/day (typically 60–80 mg/day maximum)
- Maintain at starting dose for 4–6 weeks to establish effect
- Then slow taper: reduce by 5–10 mg every 4–8 weeks based on response
- Goal: reach lowest effective maintenance dose (some patients stabilize at 5–10 mg/day)
- Total duration: individualized; some patients require years of treatment

**2. IV methylprednisolone pulses:**
- 500–1000 mg IV once daily × 3–5 consecutive days per month
- Repeated monthly or as needed
- Alternative to daily oral: may provide equivalent benefit with lower cumulative dose and different side effect profile (less systemic metabolic effects vs higher per-pulse dose)
- Requires IV access and clinic visit or home infusion nurse

**3. Oral dexamethasone pulse:**
- 40 mg/day orally × 4 consecutive days, repeated monthly
- Protocol used in some European centers and clinical trials
- May be equivalent to standard prednisone but with different side effect pattern
- Less well-studied than daily oral prednisone in CIDP specifically

### Expected Timeline

| Time | What to expect |
|------|---------------|
| 0–4 weeks | Little to no improvement (steroids work slowly) |
| 4–8 weeks | First signs of stabilization or improvement |
| 8–12 weeks | Clearer response assessment possible |
| 3–6 months | Maximum benefit from induction; consider taper |
| 6–12+ months | Ongoing taper toward lowest effective dose |

If no improvement after 3 months at adequate doses, reconsider diagnosis or add/switch treatment.

### Side Effects

Side effects are the main limitation of long-term steroid use. They are dose- and duration-dependent.

**Short-term (first weeks):**
- **Insomnia** — take steroids in the morning to minimize; avoid evening doses
- **Mood changes** — euphoria, irritability, anxiety, or depression; tell your neurologist immediately if severe
- **Increased appetite and weight gain**
- **Fluid retention / swelling** (ankles, face)
- **Elevated blood glucose** — can trigger or worsen diabetes; monitor blood sugar, especially in pre-diabetic patients
- **Elevated blood pressure**
- **Increased infection risk** — avoid sick contacts; vaccines (non-live) should be up to date before starting

**Long-term (months to years):**
- **Osteoporosis** — bone thinning and fracture risk; prevent with calcium + vitamin D supplementation, weight-bearing exercise; consider bisphosphonate if high fracture risk
- **Cataracts** (posterior subcapsular) — routine eye exams annually
- **Glaucoma** — monitor intraocular pressure
- **Cushing's syndrome** — rounded face (moon face), weight gain, stretch marks, easy bruising from prolonged high doses
- **Adrenal suppression** — long-term steroid use suppresses the adrenal glands; NEVER STOP STEROIDS ABRUPTLY (see warning below)
- **Muscle weakness (steroid myopathy)** — proximal weakness from the steroid itself; can be confused with CIDP worsening; distinguish by checking CK (normal in steroid myopathy), NCS (no change)
- **Avascular necrosis of the hip** — rare but serious; hip pain in a steroid patient warrants MRI evaluation

### Monitoring Requirements

Patients on long-term corticosteroids should have:
- Blood pressure checked at every visit
- Fasting glucose / HbA1c every 3–6 months
- Bone density (DEXA scan) at baseline and annually if on high dose
- Eye exam annually (cataracts, glaucoma)
- Height measurement annually (vertebral fracture surveillance)
- Weight monitoring

**Prophylaxis while on steroids:**
- **Calcium 1000–1200 mg/day + Vitamin D 800–1000 IU/day** — all patients on >3 months of steroids
- **Bisphosphonate** (alendronate, risedronate) — if DEXA shows osteopenia/osteoporosis or age >50
- **PPI or H2 blocker** — if taking NSAIDs concurrently (steroids alone have low GI ulcer risk)
- **Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP) prophylaxis** — if on high dose (≥20 mg prednisone/day for >1 month): trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (1 DS tablet 3×/week)

### Steroid-Sparing Agents

When long-term steroids are needed, steroid-sparing immunosuppressants allow dose reduction:

| Drug | Usual dose | Key monitoring | Notes |
|------|-----------|----------------|-------|
| Azathioprine | 1–3 mg/kg/day | CBC, LFTs monthly then q3mo | Check TPMT enzyme before starting |
| Mycophenolate mofetil | 1–3 g/day | CBC, LFTs | Generally well-tolerated |
| Methotrexate | 10–20 mg/week + folic acid | CBC, LFTs, creatinine | Weekly; avoid alcohol; folate supplementation required |
| Cyclosporine | 2–4 mg/kg/day | BP, creatinine, drug levels | Drug interactions |

Steroid-sparing agents take 3–6 months to become fully effective. Do not stop steroids before the immunosuppressant is working.

---

### Tapering Steroids Safely

- **Never stop steroids abruptly** — this can cause adrenal crisis (a medical emergency: severe weakness, nausea, vomiting, hypotension, collapse)
- Taper slowly: typically reduce by 5 mg every 4–8 weeks when stable
- If symptoms worsen during taper, return to previous dose; wait longer before tapering again
- If on ≥20 mg/day for >3 weeks, adrenal suppression has likely occurred; taper is mandatory
- At doses of 5–7.5 mg/day, most patients can eventually discontinue; do so over months

---

### Pure Motor CIDP — Critical Warning

**Do not use corticosteroids as first-line treatment for pure motor CIDP.**

Some studies and clinical experience suggest corticosteroids may **worsen** pure motor CIDP (weakness-only variant with no sensory symptoms). The mechanism is not fully understood.

- Pure motor CIDP should be treated with **IVIG** first
- If a patient's weakness increases on steroids, discuss with neurologist immediately
- Also distinguish from multifocal motor neuropathy (MMN), which is strictly pure motor and also should not receive steroids

---

## Key Points

- First-line for CIDP: strong recommendation (alongside IVIG)
- Inexpensive and widely available compared to IVIG
- Works more slowly than IVIG (weeks to months vs days to weeks)
- Long-term use requires active monitoring and side effect management
- Add calcium + Vitamin D from day one for all patients
- Never stop steroids abruptly — taper slowly under medical supervision
- Pure motor CIDP: avoid corticosteroids — risk of worsening

## Warnings / Contraindications

- **Never stop steroids abruptly** — adrenal crisis is a medical emergency (vomiting, collapse, severe hypotension); always taper
- **Pure motor CIDP**: corticosteroids may cause worsening — use IVIG instead
- **Uncontrolled diabetes or severe hypertension**: steroids can significantly worsen both — discuss risk-benefit with neurologist
- **Active serious infection**: steroids suppress immune response — treat infection before starting
- **Live vaccines** (varicella, MMR, yellow fever): contraindicated while on immunosuppressive doses of steroids; ensure vaccinations are up to date BEFORE starting treatment
