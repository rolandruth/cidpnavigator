## Summary

CIDP presents as a progressive or relapsing-remitting disorder affecting both motor (strength) and sensory (feeling) functions of the peripheral nerves. Symptoms typically develop over 8 weeks or more — unlike Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS), which peaks within 4 weeks. This document describes the full range of CIDP symptoms, how they vary by disease course and variant, and how they affect daily function.

---

## Main Content

### Core Symptoms

**Weakness (motor symptoms):**
- Typically affects both legs and arms
- Proximal (hip, shoulder) AND distal (foot, hand) weakness — distinguishes typical CIDP from length-dependent neuropathies
- Difficulty rising from a chair, climbing stairs, lifting arms overhead
- Foot drop (difficulty lifting the foot while walking)
- Grip weakness — difficulty opening jars, turning keys
- Weakness is usually **symmetric** (both sides equally) in typical CIDP
- **Asymmetric** weakness in multifocal CIDP (Lewis-Sumner / MADSAM)

**Sensory symptoms:**
- Numbness and loss of sensation (hands, feet, limbs)
- Tingling or "pins and needles" (paresthesias)
- Burning or aching pain (neuropathic pain)
- Loss of proprioception (sense of position/vibration) — leading to unsteady walking, especially in dark or on uneven ground
- Sensory symptoms often affect hands and feet first (glove-and-stocking distribution), but may extend more proximally

**Reflex loss:**
- Reduced or absent deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) in affected limbs
- Often the knee jerk and ankle jerk are reduced or absent
- Reflex loss may precede weakness

**Balance and coordination:**
- Unsteady gait, especially on uneven surfaces or in darkness
- Sensory ataxia (poor coordination due to loss of position sense, not cerebellar disease)
- Increased fall risk

### Disease Course Patterns

**1. Chronic progressive**
- Gradual worsening over months to years without significant recovery periods
- More common in older patients
- Harder to distinguish from hereditary neuropathies if onset is insidious

**2. Relapsing-remitting**
- Periods of worsening (relapses/flares) alternating with partial or full recovery
- More common in younger patients
- Relapses may be triggered by infection, surgery, or occur without identifiable cause
- Between relapses, patient may feel close to normal or have residual deficits

**3. Monophasic**
- A single episode of worsening followed by recovery
- May be difficult to distinguish from GBS (which is also monophasic but faster)
- If recovery is sustained for years, may represent "monophasic CIDP"

---

### Fatigue

- Fatigue in CIDP is distinct from muscle weakness — patients describe an overwhelming tiredness not explained by effort
- Neurological fatigue is a central feature affecting quality of life, even when motor strength appears stable
- Fatigue worsens toward the end of an IVIG dosing cycle ("IVIG wearing off")
- Often underreported by patients; ask specifically about it

---

### Pain

- Present in approximately 30–50% of patients with CIDP
- Types: burning, aching, shooting, electric-shock-like sensations
- Location: hands, feet, legs, sometimes widespread
- Pain is often neuropathic (nerve-generated) rather than musculoskeletal
- Can persist even when other symptoms are well-controlled

---

### Autonomic Symptoms (Less Common)

- Postural hypotension (dizziness on standing)
- Bladder dysfunction
- Erectile dysfunction
- These are less common than in other neuropathies but can occur in severe CIDP

---

### Cranial Nerve Involvement (Rare)

- Facial weakness
- Diplopia (double vision)
- Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
- More common in GBS than CIDP; presence should prompt reassessment of diagnosis

---

### Symptoms by CIDP Variant

| Variant | Distinctive symptoms |
|---------|---------------------|
| Typical CIDP | Symmetric proximal + distal weakness and sensory loss in all four limbs |
| Distal CIDP (DADS) | Predominantly feet/hands; little or no proximal weakness |
| Multifocal CIDP (MADSAM) | Asymmetric, patchy; one arm more affected than the other |
| Focal CIDP | Limited to one limb or plexus |
| Pure Motor CIDP | Weakness only, no numbness or tingling |
| Pure Sensory CIDP | Numbness/tingling only, no weakness; balance problems prominent |

---

### Functional Impact

The following activities are commonly affected:

- **Walking**: Foot drop, weakness, balance problems — may progress from slow walking to needing a cane, walker, or wheelchair
- **Hand function**: Grip weakness affects writing, typing, cooking, buttoning clothes
- **Stairs**: Proximal leg weakness makes stairs difficult; many patients lead with the strong leg
- **Rising from low seats**: Proximal hip weakness makes getting up from chairs, toilets, or the floor difficult
- **Driving**: Foot numbness, weakness, or delayed reaction time
- **Work**: Cognitive fog from fatigue is separate from neurological symptoms but common
- **Sleep**: Pain and restless legs can disrupt sleep

### Measuring Symptom Severity

Validated scales used by neurologists and in clinical trials:

| Scale | What it measures |
|-------|-----------------|
| **INCAT (Inflammatory Neuropathy Cause and Treatment)** | Overall disability — arm and leg function; scored 0–10 |
| **MRC Sum Score** | Muscle strength in key muscle groups; maximum 60 (higher = stronger) |
| **R-ODS (Rasch-built Overall Disability Scale)** | Activities of daily living; 0–48 |
| **grip strength (dynamometry)** | Objective hand grip force |
| **9-Hole Peg Test** | Fine motor function |
| **10-Meter Walk Test / Timed 25-Foot Walk** | Walking speed and function |

Tracking these over time helps determine whether treatment is working or disease is progressing.

---

### "IVIG Wearing Off" Phenomenon

Many patients maintained on IVIG notice symptom return in the days before their next scheduled infusion. This is called the **"wearing off" effect** and suggests:
- Maintenance interval may be too long
- Dose may need adjustment
- Or patient may be a candidate for SCIg (steady-state immunoglobulin levels without peaks/troughs)

Symptoms to track between infusions: fatigue worsening, increased weakness, numbness returning, balance deteriorating.

---

## Key Points

- Typical CIDP causes symmetric proximal AND distal weakness plus sensory loss in arms and legs
- Reflexes are reduced or absent in affected limbs
- Progression over ≥8 weeks distinguishes CIDP from GBS
- Course is progressive, relapsing-remitting, or rarely monophasic
- Fatigue is real and disabling, even when strength appears stable
- Balance problems are often due to sensory (proprioceptive) loss, not inner ear problems
- Tracking symptoms with validated scales (INCAT, MRC, grip strength) documents treatment response
- "Wearing off" before next infusion is a signal that maintenance schedule needs review

## Warnings / Contraindications

- CIDP symptoms overlap with many other conditions (GBS, MS, CMT, diabetic neuropathy, POEMS) — confirm diagnosis before attributing symptoms solely to CIDP
- Sudden severe worsening warrants urgent neurological evaluation — it may indicate relapse, intercurrent illness, or a new problem
