## Summary

Neurology appointments for CIDP are often short and densely packed. Having specific, written questions ready maximizes every minute. This document provides scenario-based question lists for the most common CIDP appointment types — from newly diagnosed to long-term management.

---

## Main Content

### How to Use These Questions

1. Print or write down your specific questions before the appointment
2. Bring them on paper — it is easy to forget questions once you're in the room
3. Prioritize: put your most important question at the top
4. Bring someone with you if possible — a second person remembers things you miss
5. Ask the nurse or front desk for extra time if you have a long list
6. Request written or patient portal follow-up for complex answers

---

### Scenario 1: Newly Diagnosed

*For the first few appointments after a CIDP diagnosis is made or suspected.*

**About the diagnosis:**
- What exactly did the nerve conduction test show that points to CIDP?
- Do I have typical CIDP or one of the variants? Which one and what does that mean?
- How confident are you in this diagnosis — is there anything that still needs to be ruled out?
- Should I be tested for paranodal antibodies (anti-NF155, anti-CNTN1, anti-CASPR1)?
- Do I need a lumbar puncture or MRI to complete the workup?
- Should I get a second opinion at a CIDP specialty center? (This is always a reasonable question.)

**About my prognosis:**
- What is my disease course likely to be — progressive, or relapsing-remitting?
- Will I recover lost function with treatment, or has some damage already become permanent?
- What is a realistic best-case and worst-case outcome?

**About starting treatment:**
- What treatment are you recommending and why for my specific case?
- What is the goal of this first treatment — stabilize, improve, or both?
- When should I expect to feel a difference?
- How will we know if the treatment is working?
- What is the plan if the first treatment doesn't work?
- Are there any reasons I specifically should NOT receive corticosteroids (e.g., if I have pure motor CIDP)?
- Should I start physical therapy at the same time?

**Practical questions:**
- How do I get this treatment — infusion center, home infusion, pharmacy?
- What will my insurance need for prior authorization? Can your office handle that?
- What do I do if I get worse before the treatment starts working?
- What symptoms should make me call you immediately vs. wait for my next appointment?

### Scenario 2: On Treatment — Monitoring Visit

*For ongoing check-in appointments while stable on IVIG, SCIg, or other treatment.*

**About treatment response:**
- Based on my nerve conduction studies / INCAT score / grip strength, is my treatment working?
- Am I at my best possible dose and schedule, or is there room to optimize?
- I notice my symptoms come back [X days] before my next infusion — does my dose or interval need adjusting?
- Is it worth trying to reduce my dose or extend my interval to see if I can use less?

**About treatment alternatives:**
- Have you heard of patients like me switching from IVIG to SCIg? Would I be a candidate?
- Is Vyvgart Hytrulo something I should consider? How do I compare to a typical ADHERE trial patient?
- Are there any new treatments I should know about?

**About long-term management:**
- Is there any chance I could eventually come off treatment — achieve remission?
- What would trigger you to recommend a different or additional treatment?
- Are there any signs of permanent damage I should be aware of?

**About daily life:**
- What exercises or physical therapy are appropriate for my current level of function?
- Are there any activities I should avoid?
- Are there any supplements that are safe (or not) with my CIDP treatment?
- Should I avoid certain vaccines while on IVIG / Vyvgart Hytrulo?

### Scenario 3: Treatment Is Not Working

*When you feel the current treatment has stopped helping or symptoms are getting worse.*

**Understanding what's happening:**
- What does the objective data show — has my INCAT score / grip strength / NCS changed?
- Is this a true treatment failure, or could my dose or frequency need adjustment?
- Could there be another explanation for my worsening — a coincidental illness, stress, or a new problem?
- Should my diagnosis be reconsidered? Could I have a paranodopathy that wouldn't respond to IVIG?

**Exploring next steps:**
- What are the options if we agree this treatment isn't adequate?
- Is plasma exchange an option for me?
- Should we add an immunosuppressant (azathioprine, mycophenolate)?
- Would a referral to a CIDP specialist or academic center be worthwhile?
- Are there any clinical trials I might be eligible for given my treatment history?

---

### Scenario 4: Considering Switching Treatments

*When exploring a change from IVIG to SCIg, or from any treatment to another.*

- What is the main reason to consider switching?
- What is the conversion process from IVIG to SCIg — how is the dose calculated?
- How will I know the new treatment is providing the same level of control?
- What happens if the new treatment doesn't work as well — can I go back?
- What insurance steps are needed to approve the new treatment?
- Who trains me on SCIg injection technique and pump operation?
- How long will it take to transition and stabilize on the new treatment?

---

### Scenario 5: Insurance and Cost Concerns

*When IVIG or other treatment is unaffordable or coverage is being denied.*

- My insurance is denying IVIG / requiring step therapy first. What can you put in an appeal letter?
- Can I get the same benefit with a less expensive treatment option?
- Would SCIg solve my coverage problem (different insurance benefit — Part D vs. Part B)?
- Are there manufacturer patient assistance programs you can connect me with?
- Is there a clinical trial that would provide my treatment at no cost?
- Can your office submit a peer-to-peer review call on my behalf?

---

### Scenario 6: Clinical Trial Interest

- Am I a good candidate for any currently enrolling CIDP trials?
- What would participation involve — how many visits, what procedures?
- Would I have to stop my current treatment to participate?
- What happens to my care if I'm randomized to placebo?
- How do I find out if there are trials near me?
- Who do I contact at your practice if a trial becomes available?

---

### Scenario 7: Seeking a Second Opinion

It is always reasonable to seek a second opinion for CIDP. Asking your current neurologist is not an insult — it is standard practice for complex diseases.

- Would you be willing to refer me to a CIDP specialist or academic medical center for a second opinion?
- Is there a GBS/CIDP Foundation Center of Excellence near me?
- What records should I bring to a second opinion appointment?

---

## Key Points

- Print questions before your appointment — it is easy to forget them once you're in the room
- Put your most important question first in case time runs out
- Bring a companion who can take notes
- Ask for patient portal or written follow-up for complex or important answers
- Seeking a second opinion is always appropriate and should not be discouraged by any neurologist

## Warnings / Contraindications

None — these are conversational preparation guides, not medical recommendations.
