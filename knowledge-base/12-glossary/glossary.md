## Summary

Plain-language definitions of medical and insurance terms commonly encountered by CIDP patients. Designed for patient audiences, not medical professionals. Where appropriate, the full term and common abbreviation are both listed.

---

## Condition-Related Terms

**CIDP (Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy)**
A long-term disease in which the immune system attacks the myelin (protective coating) around the peripheral nerves. This slows or blocks nerve signals, causing weakness, numbness, tingling, and loss of reflexes. CIDP is chronic — it does not go away on its own and usually requires ongoing treatment.

**GBS (Guillain-Barré Syndrome)**
A related but different nerve disorder in which the immune system attacks the peripheral nerves rapidly — usually peaking within 4 weeks. Unlike CIDP, GBS is typically a one-time episode from which most people partially or fully recover. Sometimes CIDP is initially mistaken for GBS.

**Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)**
The nerves outside the brain and spinal cord. This includes the nerves that go to the arms, legs, and organs. CIDP affects the peripheral nervous system — not the brain or spinal cord (which are the central nervous system, or CNS).

**Demyelination**
Damage to or loss of the myelin sheath — the protective insulating coating around nerve fibers. In CIDP, the immune system attacks myelin. Without myelin, nerve signals slow down or fail to reach their destination, causing weakness and sensory loss.

**Myelin**
The fatty, white protective sheath that wraps around nerve fibers like insulation around an electrical wire. It allows nerve signals to travel quickly. When myelin is damaged (demyelinated), signals slow or stop.

**Axon**
The long, thin part of a nerve cell (neuron) that carries electrical signals. In early CIDP, axons are usually preserved while myelin is damaged. With severe or long-standing disease, axons themselves can be damaged — which leads to more permanent weakness and slower recovery.

**Autoimmune**
A condition in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues. In CIDP, the immune system targets myelin in the peripheral nerves.

**Neuropathy**
A general term for disease or damage to the nerves. "Peripheral neuropathy" means nerve disease outside the brain and spinal cord.

**Polyneuropathy**
Neuropathy affecting multiple nerves throughout the body simultaneously ("poly" = many). CIDP is a polyneuropathy.

**Polyradiculoneuropathy**
Neuropathy affecting both nerve roots (the part of the nerve near the spinal cord) and peripheral nerves. The full name of CIDP — "chronic inflammatory demyelinating *polyradiculoneuropathy*" — reflects this.

**Areflexia**
Absent or very reduced reflexes (like the knee-jerk reflex). Common in CIDP because the reflex arc runs through demyelinated peripheral nerves.

**Ataxia**
Poor coordination and unsteady movement. In CIDP, ataxia is often caused by sensory loss (particularly proprioception), not by brain problems.

**Proprioception**
The sense of where your body parts are in space — knowing where your feet are without looking. Damage to sensory nerves in CIDP often affects proprioception, making walking on uneven ground or in the dark difficult.

**Relapse (Flare)**
A return or worsening of CIDP symptoms after a period of stability or improvement. Can be triggered by infection or occur without a clear cause.

**Remission**
A period when CIDP symptoms are minimal or absent, with or without ongoing treatment. Some patients achieve drug-free remission.

**MADSAM (Multifocal Acquired Demyelinating Sensory and Motor Neuropathy)**
Also called Lewis-Sumner syndrome. A CIDP variant with patchy, asymmetric nerve involvement (affects some limbs more than others). Treated similarly to typical CIDP.

**MMN (Multifocal Motor Neuropathy)**
A separate condition that causes weakness only, with no sensory symptoms, in an asymmetric pattern. Often confused with CIDP or pure motor CIDP. MMN should NOT be treated with corticosteroids. Treated with IVIG.

**Paranodopathy**
A group of conditions related to CIDP but caused by antibodies attacking specific proteins at the node of Ranvier (the junction between myelin segments on a nerve). Paranodopathies include anti-NF155, anti-CNTN1, and anti-CASPR1 antibody conditions. They often respond poorly to IVIG but may respond to rituximab.

## Diagnostic Terms

**NCS (Nerve Conduction Study)**
A test that measures the speed and strength of electrical signals traveling through a nerve. In CIDP, signals are slower than normal because myelin is damaged. A key diagnostic test.

**EMG (Electromyography)**
A test that measures the electrical activity of muscles. Often done alongside NCS. Helps distinguish nerve problems from muscle problems.

**CSF (Cerebrospinal Fluid)**
The clear fluid surrounding the brain and spinal cord. In CIDP, CSF protein is often elevated (>45 mg/dL) while cell count is normal. Obtained via lumbar puncture (spinal tap).

**Lumbar Puncture (Spinal Tap)**
A procedure to collect CSF by inserting a needle into the lower back. Used to measure CSF protein in the workup for CIDP.

**INCAT Score (Inflammatory Neuropathy Cause and Treatment)**
A disability scale used in CIDP trials and clinical practice. Rates arm and leg disability from 0 (no disability) to 10 (maximum disability). A 1-point improvement is considered clinically meaningful.

**MRC Sum Score**
A strength measurement scale (Medical Research Council). Tests strength in key muscle groups on both sides; maximum score of 60 = normal strength. Lower scores = more weakness.

**DEXA Scan (Dual-energy X-ray Absorptiometry)**
A bone density scan used to monitor for osteoporosis, especially in patients on long-term corticosteroids.

## Treatment Terms

**IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)**
A treatment made from pooled human plasma antibodies (IgG). Given by IV infusion. First-line for CIDP. Helps by temporarily suppressing the immune attack on myelin.

**SCIg (Subcutaneous Immunoglobulin)**
The same IgG as IVIG but delivered by small injections under the skin, at home, by the patient. FDA-approved brands: Hizentra and Cuvitru. Used for CIDP maintenance.

**IgG (Immunoglobulin G)**
The most common type of antibody in the blood. IVIG and SCIg are made of IgG. In CIDP, certain IgG antibodies attack myelin; IVIG treatment temporarily suppresses this process.

**IgA (Immunoglobulin A)**
A different type of antibody. Patients with IgA deficiency may have severe allergic reactions to IVIG and should not receive it. All patients should be screened for IgA deficiency before first IVIG infusion.

**FcRn (Neonatal Fc Receptor)**
A receptor in the body that recycles IgG antibodies, keeping them in circulation longer. Vyvgart Hytrulo (efgartigimod) blocks this receptor to speed up clearance of all IgG, including the harmful antibodies that cause CIDP.

**Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis / PLEX)**
A procedure that removes the liquid part of blood (plasma, which contains harmful antibodies) and replaces it with clean fluid. Used for CIDP when IVIG and steroids don't work or are contraindicated.

**Corticosteroids (Steroids)**
Anti-inflammatory medications that suppress the immune system. Used for CIDP: prednisone (oral), methylprednisolone (IV), dexamethasone (oral pulse). Different from anabolic steroids used by athletes — these are "stress hormones."

**Prednisone**
The most commonly used oral corticosteroid for CIDP. Generic, inexpensive, and effective. Requires slow tapering — do not stop abruptly.

**Methylprednisolone**
A corticosteroid given by IV infusion (brand name: Solu-Medrol). Used as pulse therapy for CIDP.

**Immunosuppressant**
A drug that reduces immune system activity. Used in CIDP to reduce steroid requirements (steroid-sparing). Examples: azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, methotrexate.

**Azathioprine (Imuran)**
An immunosuppressant used as a steroid-sparing agent in CIDP. Takes 3–6 months to reach full effect. Requires regular blood count monitoring.

**Mycophenolate Mofetil (CellCept)**
An immunosuppressant often used as steroid-sparing in CIDP. Generally well-tolerated.

**Rituximab (Rituxan)**
A biologic medication (monoclonal antibody) that depletes B cells. Not in the 2021 CIDP guideline for typical CIDP, but used off-label. Strongly indicated for paranodopathy patients (anti-NF155, anti-CNTN1).

**Efgartigimod (Vyvgart Hytrulo)**
The brand name subcutaneous FcRn blocker approved for CIDP in June 2024. Once-weekly injection. Reduces all circulating IgG levels.

**Loading Dose (Induction Dose)**
The initial high dose of a medication used to quickly reach therapeutic levels. For IVIG in CIDP: 2 g/kg total over 2–5 days.

**Maintenance Dose**
The ongoing lower dose given after the loading dose to maintain the therapeutic effect. For IVIG in CIDP: typically 1 g/kg every 3 weeks.

**Step Therapy (Fail First)**
An insurance requirement that patients try less expensive treatments before the insurer will cover more expensive ones. Example: requiring a trial of corticosteroids before approving IVIG.

## Insurance Terms

**Prior Authorization (Prior Auth / PA)**
Permission from your insurance company required before they will cover a specific treatment or medication. Neurologists must submit medical documentation to obtain approval.

**Medical Necessity**
A standard insurers use to determine if a treatment is appropriate and needed for a patient's condition. Demonstrating medical necessity is key to getting IVIG, SCIg, and Vyvgart Hytrulo covered.

**Appeal**
A formal request to your insurance company to reconsider a denial of coverage. Appeals should include medical records, physician letters, and guideline citations.

**Peer-to-Peer Review**
A process where your neurologist speaks directly with the insurance company's medical reviewer to advocate for your treatment. Often required for prior authorization disputes.

**Formulary**
The list of drugs your insurance plan covers. IVIG is usually on formulary as a medical benefit; SCIg may be on the pharmacy formulary under different terms.

**Medicare Part B**
The part of Medicare that covers medical services and procedures, including IVIG administered in a clinic or at home by a nurse.

**Medicare Part D**
The part of Medicare that covers prescription drugs. SCIg is typically covered under Part D rather than Part B — this affects out-of-pocket costs.

**Specialty Pharmacy**
A pharmacy that specializes in high-cost, complex medications (like IVIG, SCIg, Vyvgart Hytrulo). Your neurologist will usually route these prescriptions to a specialty pharmacy.

**Co-pay Assistance**
Financial support from drug manufacturers or non-profit foundations to help cover the patient's share of treatment costs. Available for most CIDP treatments.
