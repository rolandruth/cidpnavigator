## Summary

The 2021 EAN/PNS guideline provides evidence-based treatment recommendations for CIDP. First-line treatment is IVIG or corticosteroids (both strongly recommended). Plasma exchange is recommended for patients who fail or cannot receive first-line treatments. Long-term maintenance with IVIG, SCIg, or steroids is needed for most patients. Treatment goals are stabilization, improvement in function, and eventually achieving remission. A new FDA-approved option (Vyvgart Hytrulo / efgartigimod) became available in 2024 (not in the 2021 guideline).

---

## Main Content

### Treatment Goals

1. **Stabilize** — stop progression
2. **Improve** — regain lost function
3. **Maintain** — prevent relapse during maintenance treatment
4. **Taper/withdraw** — attempt to identify the lowest effective dose or achieve drug-free remission
5. **Monitor for paranodopathy** — some patients (anti-NF155, anti-CNTN1, anti-CASPR1 positive) need different treatments

---

### First-Line Treatment Options

The guideline gives **strong recommendations (Level A)** for three first-line approaches:

#### Option 1: IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)
- **Induction**: 2 g/kg total dose over 2–5 days
- **Maintenance**: 1 g/kg every 3 weeks OR 2 g/kg every 6 weeks
- Onset of benefit: within days to 2 weeks
- Evidence level: highest (multiple randomized controlled trials, Cochrane reviews)
- Response rate: approximately 60–70% of patients
- Also available as subcutaneous (SCIg) for maintenance — see `05-scig/`

#### Option 2: Corticosteroids
- **Oral prednisone**: typically 1 mg/kg/day (maximum 80 mg/day) for 4–6 weeks, then slow taper
- **IV methylprednisolone pulses**: 500–1000 mg/day × 3–5 days per month (alternative to daily oral)
- **Dexamethasone pulse**: 40 mg/day × 4 days per month (some centers use this protocol)
- Onset of benefit: slower than IVIG (weeks to months for full effect)
- Evidence level: strong; long clinical history
- Response rate: similar to IVIG (~60–70%) but different side effect profile
- Not preferred for pure motor CIDP (may worsen)

#### Option 3: Plasma Exchange (PE / PLEX)
- Used as first-line when IVIG and steroids are contraindicated, or as acute treatment
- Also used as **second-line** when IVIG and steroids fail
- Protocol: typically 3–5 exchanges over 1–2 weeks for acute benefit
- Effect is short-lived; requires repeated sessions for maintenance
- Evidence level: strong (similar to IVIG in short-term trials)

### Choosing Between First-Line Options

| Consideration | Favors IVIG | Favors Steroids |
|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Speed of benefit needed | Yes (faster) | — |
| Pure motor CIDP | Yes (steroids may worsen) | No |
| Long-term cost concern | — | Yes (steroids are inexpensive) |
| Frailty / cardiovascular risk | — | Caution (steroids raise BP, glucose) |
| Osteoporosis risk | — | Caution (long-term steroids) |
| IgA deficiency | Contraindicated | Yes |
| Preference for oral treatment | — | Yes |
| Adequate venous access | Needed | Not required |
| Relapsing-remitting course | Either | Either |

### Maintenance Treatment

Most patients require long-term maintenance therapy. After response to induction:

**IVIG maintenance:**
- Titrate to lowest effective dose and longest interval
- Typical maintenance: 1 g/kg every 3–6 weeks (individualized)
- SCIg is an equivalent alternative for maintenance (home-based)

**Steroid maintenance:**
- Taper oral prednisone slowly (e.g., reduce by 5 mg every 4–8 weeks)
- Goal: lowest dose that maintains function
- Add steroid-sparing immunosuppressants to allow further steroid reduction

**SCIg maintenance:**
- FDA-approved (Hizentra, Cuvitru) as maintenance alternative to IVIG
- Lower volume, weekly self-injection at home
- Steadier serum IgG levels — reduces "wearing off"
- See `05-scig/` for details

### Second-Line and Add-On Treatments

When first-line treatment provides partial response or cannot be tolerated:

**Immunosuppressants (steroid-sparing agents):**

| Drug | Starting dose (typical) | Use case |
|------|------------------------|---------|
| Azathioprine | 1–3 mg/kg/day | Add to steroids to allow dose reduction |
| Mycophenolate mofetil | 1–3 g/day divided | Steroid-sparing; better tolerated |
| Methotrexate | 10–20 mg/week | Steroid-sparing |
| Cyclosporine | 2–4 mg/kg/day | Refractory cases |
| Cyclophosphamide | Variable | Severe refractory cases only |

**Evidence note:** Immunosuppressants in CIDP have limited high-quality evidence. They are used to reduce steroid requirements rather than as standalone treatments.

**Rituximab:**
- Not in the 2021 guideline for typical CIDP, but used off-label in refractory cases
- **Strongly indicated** for paranodopathy patients (anti-NF155, anti-CNTN1, anti-CASPR1 antibodies)
- Mechanism: depletes B cells
- Requires monitoring for PML and infections

### New Treatment: Efgartigimod (Vyvgart Hytrulo) — 2024

*Added post-guideline — FDA approved June 21, 2024:*

- FcRn blocker — reduces circulating pathogenic IgG antibodies (different mechanism from IVIG)
- Subcutaneous injection, once weekly
- ADHERE trial: 69% of patients showed clinical improvement; 61% reduction in relapse risk vs placebo
- First new mechanism of action for CIDP treatment in decades
- See `06-vyvgart-hytrulo/` for full details

### Treatment Response Assessment

**How to assess response:**
- Measure function at baseline (INCAT, MRC sum score, grip strength) **before** starting treatment
- Reassess at 3–6 months
- A ≥1-point improvement in INCAT is considered clinically meaningful
- Lack of response after 3–6 months at adequate doses suggests:
  - Wrong diagnosis (reassess)
  - Paranodopathy (check antibodies)
  - Need to switch to alternative first-line or add second-line agent

**Clinical response red flags:**
- No improvement in IVIG after two full induction courses → consider alternative diagnosis or paranodopathy panel
- Worsening on corticosteroids → consider pure motor CIDP variant
- Rapid relapse at every treatment cycle → evaluate for more frequent dosing or switch to SCIg

---

### Treatment Withdrawal / Remission

- Some patients achieve remission and can discontinue treatment
- Guideline recommends attempting gradual taper after 3–6 months of stable function
- 20–30% of CIDP patients may achieve sustained remission off treatment
- Relapse risk remains — monitor every 3–6 months after stopping
- Restart treatment promptly if relapse occurs — do not delay

### Variant-Specific Treatment Notes

| Variant | Treatment considerations |
|---------|------------------------|
| Typical CIDP | IVIG or steroids (either); plasma exchange if both fail |
| Distal CIDP (DADS) | IVIG preferred; check anti-MAG (if positive — separate entity, treat differently) |
| Multifocal CIDP (MADSAM) | IVIG preferred; steroids less evidence for multifocal variant |
| Pure Motor CIDP | IVIG strongly preferred; avoid steroids (may worsen) |
| Pure Sensory CIDP | IVIG or steroids; plasma exchange if needed |
| Paranodopathies (anti-NF155/CNTN1/CASPR1) | Rituximab; poor IVIG response expected |

## Key Points

- Strong recommendation: IVIG or corticosteroids as first-line for typical CIDP
- Plasma exchange is first-line when IVIG/steroids contraindicated, otherwise second-line
- SCIg is an equally effective, home-based maintenance alternative to IV IVIG
- Pure motor CIDP: avoid corticosteroids — use IVIG
- Paranodopathy antibody-positive patients: use rituximab, not IVIG
- Efgartigimod (Vyvgart Hytrulo) is FDA-approved (June 2024) as a new subcutaneous FcRn-blocking option
- About 20–30% of patients can achieve treatment-free remission with careful tapering
- Always measure baseline function before starting treatment so response can be documented

## Warnings / Contraindications

- IgA deficiency: absolute contraindication to IVIG (use steroids or plasma exchange instead)
- Pure motor CIDP: steroids may worsen the condition — discuss with neurologist before using
- Anti-MAG neuropathy: does not respond to standard CIDP treatments — requires different management
- Never stop steroids abruptly (risk of adrenal crisis) — always taper under medical supervision
- Immunosuppressants require regular blood count monitoring (risk of bone marrow suppression, liver toxicity)
